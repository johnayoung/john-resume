Agentic AI changes the build-vs-buy decision because it lowers the cost of building. It does not erase the cost of owning.

That distinction matters. A lot of software that used to be obviously too expensive to build is now plausible. Internal tools, workflow glue, admin surfaces, reporting pipelines, onboarding flows, integrations, small CRMs, data cleanup systems, and customer-specific automations are all closer to "reasonable weekend prototype" than "quarter-long engineering project."

The old question was often: **can we afford to build this?**

The new question is sharper: **is this worth owning?**

## The Take

Agentic AI shifts the boundary toward building when the problem is close to your business and the requirements are weird in ways that matter.

It shifts less when the problem is generic, compliance-heavy, operationally sensitive, or already solved by a mature vendor.

In other words: AI makes custom software cheaper. It does not make custom software free.

## When Building Gets More Attractive

Build becomes more interesting when:

- The workflow is specific to how your company operates.
- The off-the-shelf tool forces process changes you do not want.
- The feature set you need is small, but the vendor product is broad and expensive.
- The integration surface is mostly internal.
- The value comes from speed of iteration, not from a vendor's generic feature depth.
- The system can be verified with tests, logs, metrics, and human review.

This is where agents are genuinely disruptive. They reduce the tax on boring but necessary software: the forms, scripts, dashboards, migrations, glue code, and integration work that used to be painful enough to justify another SaaS subscription.

## When Buying Still Wins

Buy still wins when:

- The problem is not a source of differentiation.
- The vendor carries risk you do not want to own.
- Reliability, uptime, support, security, audits, or compliance are the real product.
- The domain changes constantly and someone else is paid to track it.
- The cost of being wrong is high.
- Your team would become the support desk for an internal product nobody wants to maintain.

Agentic AI can help you build a payroll system faster. That does not mean you should own payroll correctness, tax tables, compliance updates, security posture, and support burden.

## The New Decision Rule

The build-vs-buy decision used to overweight implementation cost because implementation was the obvious bottleneck. Agentic AI lowers that bottleneck, so the hidden costs become more visible.

Ask:

1. Does owning this system create strategic leverage?
2. Are our requirements meaningfully different from the market default?
3. Can we verify the system cheaply and continuously?
4. Can we maintain it without creating a shadow product team?
5. Will the agent help with future changes, or only the first build?

If the answer is mostly yes, agentic AI may push the decision toward build.

If the answer is mostly no, the agent is just making it easier to create a liability.

## Possible Angle

The interesting change is not "AI means everyone should build more software." The interesting change is that the economic boundary moved. Small, business-specific software used to die in the gap between "too annoying to do manually" and "not valuable enough to staff." Agentic AI shrinks that gap.

That creates a new category of build-worthy software: tools that are too specific for SaaS, too valuable for spreadsheets, and now cheap enough to own.
