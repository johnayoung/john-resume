Frontier agents get more capable on a schedule you can graph. The length of task a model can finish at 50% reliability — measured in the human time the task takes, not how long the agent runs unattended ([Grace Huckins / MIT Technology Review: This is the most misunderstood graph in AI](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/02/05/1132254/this-is-the-most-misunderstood-graph-in-ai/)) — has been [doubling approximately every seven months since 2019](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14499) ([Kwa, West, Becker et al., METR](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14499)), and METR's latest methodology estimates the recent trend is faster still: a [131-day doubling since 2023 and an 89-day doubling since 2024 under TH1.1](https://metr.org/blog/2026-1-29-time-horizon-1-1/). Read the curve alone and the strategy writes itself: point agents at more work, wait for the next model, repeat.

Then the one controlled field trial that measured delivered output found the opposite. On real issues in repositories they knew deeply, [experienced developers took **19% longer** with AI tools](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) — and the part that should unsettle you is the perception gap: they had forecast a 24% speedup and, after finishing slower, still believed AI had sped them up by 20%. I am going to update that number in the open, because no vendor report in this space does. METR itself now flags its follow-up data as [*"an unreliable signal of the current productivity effect of AI tools"*](https://metr.org/blog/2026-02-24-uplift-update/), estimates newly-recruited developers at −4% (a confidence interval from −15% to +9%), and expects the magnitude has shifted as the tools improved. Even sympathetic critics grant the trial's rigor while noting its setting was near-worst-case — [deeply understood open-source repos are "close to a worst-case scenario for AI tools"](https://thezvi.substack.com/p/on-metrs-ai-coding-rct) ([Zvi Mowshowitz](https://thezvi.substack.com/p/on-metrs-ai-coding-rct)). So treat the slowdown as a point-in-time, early-2025 snapshot. The durable finding is the perception gap — measured productivity and gut feel pointing in opposite directions — and that is a systems problem no model release fixes.

Both numbers are real, and the space between them is what this report is about. Generation got cheap. The cost of shipping software did not vanish with it — it moved **downstream**, onto the work that was always the expensive part and is now the binding constraint: deciding what to delegate, budgeting the context an agent reads, proving output you didn't write, containing what it can reach, absorbing its diffs through a review pool that didn't grow, and owning the result for the years after it merges. Addy Osmani compressed the whole thesis into one line: [*"We made writing cheap, and understanding stayed exactly as expensive as it has always been."*](https://www.oreilly.com/radar/agentic-code-review/)

The institutions are catching up to it. Google's 2025 DORA report — the largest study of its kind, and a reversal of its own 2024 read — now finds a [positive relationship between AI adoption and delivery throughput, while AI adoption "does continue to have a negative relationship with software delivery stability"](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/announcing-the-2025-dora-report). Its own framing is the thesis in institutional language: [*"AI doesn't fix a team; it amplifies what's already there."*](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/announcing-the-2025-dora-report) Cheap generation magnifies a strong system and a weak one alike.

To keep this concrete, follow one artifact the whole way down. Picture a single agent-authored pull request opened this morning: a 190-line diff that adds a Redis cache to a `/reports` endpoint, with a description promising it invalidates the cache on write. Each of the six pillars below is a station that diff passes through — the choice to delegate it, the context the agent read to write it, whether its description matches its code, the queue it lands in, and the tail you own after it merges. Each pillar gets the strongest primary evidence I could find, then the judgment I'd act on. The evidence base is public: a [verified index of primary sources](/research/), every row a verbatim figure or quote checked against its source, updated as I publish and as the numbers move. This is the argument that index adds up to.

## Pillar 1 — Task Design & Decomposition

**The claim: what you delegate matters more than which agent you delegate to, and the property that decides it is verification cost, not size.**

The internet has a thousand "best AI coding agent" rankings and near-zero guidance on the only decision upstream of them: does this task belong to an agent at all? Two properties settle it — can you verify the result cheaply, and can you undo it if you can't. Anthropic leads its own agent-building guidance with the same discipline, naming the option a ranking never will: [*"This might mean not building agentic systems at all."*](https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents)

The reliability floor is measurable and steep. METR found current models have [near-100% success on tasks under four minutes of human time, but under 10% past roughly four hours](https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/) — success doesn't decay gently with task length, it collapses. That hands you a portable rule keyed to the one estimate you can make before writing a spec: how long would this take a competent human? Hours means it isn't one task, and handing it over whole is betting against a sub-10% success rate.

But duration is the easy axis. The subtler one is verification cost, and it's where the measured slowdown came from. Osmani's survey data shows [only 48% of developers consistently check AI-assisted code before committing, even though 38% find reviewing AI logic *harder* than reviewing human code](https://addyo.substack.com/p/the-80-problem-in-agentic-coding). When proving the diff costs more than writing it, delegation is a detour with a review bill at the end. Our `/reports` caching PR exists only because someone chose to delegate its task — and cache invalidation is the trap this pillar is built to catch: cheap for an agent to write, expensive for a human to prove correct. You cannot cheaply confirm a cache stays coherent under concurrent writes. That is exactly the task to score *before* the prompt, not after the diff lands.

**The judgment: score the task on verification cost and blast radius before you open a prompt, and size to a reviewable unit — not a line count.** The delegation question is therefore: can you hand the agent a check *it* can run — a test, a build, a diff-against-fixture — so it stops treating "looks done" as the finish line? Refuse long-horizon unbounded work and decompose it into sub-hour, independently verifiable pieces. Keep vague, taste-heavy, or context-in-your-head work in your own hands, because that is precisely where the stopwatch went negative. Sizing is a context problem — the constraint is how much the agent must hold in its window to finish cleanly, not how many lines come out. (Deep dives: [What AI Coding Agents Are Actually Good For](/blog/what-ai-agents-are-actually-good-for/), [How to Size Tasks for AI Coding Agents](/blog/how-to-size-tasks-for-ai-coding-agents/), and [The Anatomy of a Perfect AI Agent Task](/blog/anatomy-of-a-perfect-ai-agent-task/).)

One number keeps the fear calibrated: in real agent traffic Anthropic measured [only 0.8% of actions as irreversible, with 80% of tool calls carrying at least one safeguard](https://www.anthropic.com/news/measuring-agent-autonomy). Oversight should concentrate on that thin irreversible slice where a single error is catastrophic — not spread flat across the 99% a rerun fixes.

## Pillar 2 — Agent Runtime

**The claim: context is a budget you spend against a hard ceiling, not a free input you maximize.**

The instinct is to pour everything into the window — the whole CLAUDE.md, every tool, all the retrieved docs — on the theory that more information can only help. Past a threshold the evidence says the opposite. Distyl's density study found [even the best frontier models hit only **68% accuracy at 500 instructions**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.11538), and that the errors are lopsided: ["Models overwhelmingly err toward omission errors as instruction density increases"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.11538) ([Jaroslawicz, Whiting, Shah, Maamari, Distyl AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.11538)) — the rule you add doesn't get followed loosely, it gets silently dropped. Chroma's context-rot work across 18 models found that [models "do not use their context uniformly; instead, their performance grows increasingly unreliable as input length grows"](https://www.trychroma.com/research/context-rot). A longer CLAUDE.md is not a neutral cost. It is a reliability tax you pay whether or not you notice.

There is a concrete ceiling to budget against. HumanLayer puts frontier instruction-following at [*"~ 150-200 instructions with reasonable consistency"*, with the Claude Code system prompt already spending ~50 of them](https://www.humanlayer.dev/blog/writing-a-good-claude-md) before your project file adds a word. The tool layer degrades the same way: Anthropic reports [the most common failures are "wrong tool selection and incorrect parameters, especially when tools have similar names"](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/advanced-tool-use) like `notification-send-user` versus `notification-send-channel` — which is why just-in-time tool loading both cuts token cost and *raises* selection accuracy. Just-in-time retrieval isn't free either; Anthropic is explicit that [*"runtime exploration is slower than retrieving pre-computed data"*](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/effective-context-engineering-for-ai-agents), so you have to design the fallback for when it misses.

This is the station where our caching PR was quietly under-budgeted. What the agent read to write it was a spend, not a dump — the endpoint, the ORM layer, the cache config, drawn into a window already carrying that ~50-instruction system prompt and however long your CLAUDE.md runs. And an empirical study of real context files found the systematically missing half: [teams write them to make agents functional but "provide few guardrails to ensure that agent-written code is secure or performant"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.12884). The done-condition that would have forced the invalidation step — "a write must evict the cached key" — is exactly the kind of constraint those files omit.

**The judgment: treat CLAUDE.md like maintained config against an instruction ceiling, not a README you append to forever.** Budget it. Every instruction competes for the same finite compliance, so the marginal rule you add lowers adherence to the ones already there — the difference between shipping clean on the first try and shipping a plausible near-miss. Prefer just-in-time retrieval over eager stuffing, but design the fallback, because retrieval failures are silent by default. And spend your instruction budget on the constraints — security, performance, done-conditions — not just the setup, since that is the half nobody writes. (Deep dives: [The CLAUDE.md Instruction Ceiling](/blog/claude-md-instruction-ceiling/) and [Where Just-in-Time Context Retrieval Silently Breaks](/blog/jit-context-retrieval-failure/).)

## Pillar 3 — Evals & Verification

**The claim: generation outran verification, and a green build is the weakest signal you own.**

This is the load-bearing pillar — the one the whole thesis rests on. The academic framing is a [*"speed vs. trust"* gap](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.06216) ([Hassan et al.](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.06216)): agent hyper-productivity produces PRs that look merge-ready and aren't. On audit the gap is brutal — one study found [true solve rates for GPT-4 patches "dropped from 12.47% to 3.97% after detailed manual audits, revealing widespread weak or cosmetic solutions"](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.06216) (citing Aleithan et al. 2024). And the single most common defect is one no test suite catches. Studying agent-authored PRs, researchers found that [*"descriptions claim unimplemented changes"* was the most common issue at **45.4%**, and high-mismatch PRs had 51.7% lower acceptance rates and took 3.5x longer to merge](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04886) ([Gong, Pinna, Bian, Zhang](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04886)).

That statistic is our caching PR's station exactly. Its description promises invalidation-on-write; its code wires the cache but never wires the eviction — the story and the diff diverge, and the tests still pass because no test asserts the promise. Closing that gap is human work. It also explains why the debt is so easy to miss: New Relic's 2026 survey of 200 technology leaders (run by Hanover Research, so read it as reported perception, not incident telemetry) captures the pattern where [94% rate AI-generated code as higher quality than human-authored code *at the moment it is reviewed*](https://newrelic.com/blog/ai/state-of-ai-coding-2026), even as the same leaders report [AI code introduces "roughly 1.7 times more critical runtime issues"](https://newrelic.com/blog/ai/state-of-ai-coding-2026) and [78% saw a measurable spike in production incidents tied to AI code](https://newrelic.com/blog/ai/state-of-ai-coding-2026). The code looks best exactly where it is checked and fails where it isn't — a stale cache reads clean in review and breaks at 2am.

The reflexive proxies for quality don't hold. Benchmark scores are the worst offender: a Berkeley audit built a scanning agent and found that across [eight prominent benchmarks — SWE-bench, WebArena, OSWorld, GAIA, and others — "every single one can be exploited to achieve near-perfect scores without solving a single task"](https://rdi.berkeley.edu/blog/trustworthy-benchmarks-cont). Even *model* comparisons are noisier than they look — Anthropic notes [*"a 2-point lead on a leaderboard might reflect a genuine capability difference, or it might reflect that one eval ran on beefier hardware, or even at a luckier time of day"*](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/infrastructure-noise). Nor does "have the agents review the agents" close the loop yet: on a human-annotated benchmark, [8 frontier models detect only 15-31% of human-flagged issues](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.26130) ([Kumar, SWE-PRBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.26130)). The field has already priced this in — [more developers actively distrust the accuracy of AI tools (46%) than trust it (33%)](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2025/ai) ([Stack Overflow 2025 Developer Survey](https://survey.stackoverflow.co/2025/ai)).

**The judgment: build verification as a per-task discipline that assumes the agent's self-report is unreliable, and calibrate scrutiny to blast radius.** Distrust the description — diff it against the code, because the 45.4% claim-mismatch rate makes the PR narrative the least trustworthy artifact in the review. Treat a passing suite as necessary, never sufficient, and grade what the agent produced rather than the path it took ([Anthropic: Demystifying evals for AI agents](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/demystifying-evals-for-ai-agents)). Ignore leaderboards for anything but coarse triage. Spend your review budget where a wrong result is expensive to undo, not evenly across every diff. Willison names the discipline underneath all of this: the [defining feature of a coding agent is that it can "both generate and _execute_ code"](https://simonw.substack.com/p/agentic-engineering-patterns) — so the loop, and the check that closes it, is the job. Generation is cheap now; verification is the entire remaining job. (Deep dive: [How to Verify AI Coding Agent Output](/blog/evaluating-ai-coding-agent-output/).)

## Pillar 4 — Production Operations

**The claim: you supervise what an agent *can reach*, not what it does — and you can't cap a cost you can't attribute.**

Watching an agent's behavior is a losing game, because any model-layer check has a non-zero miss rate and a determined-enough agent finds the gap. Anthropic makes the shift explicit: [*"Rather than supervising what the agent does, we supervise what it's able to do by enforcing access boundaries"*](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/how-we-contain-claude) — sandboxes, VMs, egress controls — and reports an [84% reduction in permission prompts](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/how-we-contain-claude) once the boundary moved to the OS. AWS names the failure this prevents: [*excessive agency*](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/wellarchitected/latest/generative-ai-lens/gensec05-bp01.html), an agent taking broader actions than its scope, countered with least-privilege and confirmation on the sharp edges. The production-database deletions of the past year bear it out. In the PocketOS incident, [a Railway CLI token scoped for custom domains "had blanket permissions across the entire GraphQL API, including destructive operations on production volumes"](https://zenity.io/blog/current-events/ai-agent-database-deletion-pocketos) — a nine-second deletion against a three-month-old backup. A blanket token is a footgun regardless of which model holds it, which is why even the [vendor defense in the Amazon case framed it as "user error — specifically misconfigured access controls — not AI"](https://blog.barrack.ai/amazon-ai-agents-deleting-production). These are authorization failures wearing the costume of model failures.

Autonomy, then, isn't a switch you flip on day one. Monte Carlo frames it as [a score "that goes up or down"](https://montecarlo.ai/blog-agentic-autonomy-is-a-trust-score) — earned through demonstrated reliability in your specific environment, and revocable when it slips.

The cost side has the same shape and is even less solved. The running example steps aside here: this station is about the token the agent holds and the meter on its calls, not the caching diff itself. Provider dashboards are the wrong instrument — Anthropic's own analytics docs warn that [values for a given date "can be revised for up to 30 days"](https://platform.claude.com/docs/en/manage-claude/analytics-api) and are attributed per-user, not per-request. A number that moves for a month and can't see a single task is a lagging report, not a guardrail. The genuinely hard problem, per the FinOps Foundation, is [*"identifying the consumer of the model output"*](https://www.finops.org/wg/finops-for-ai-overview/), which multi-agent systems make acute — there is no accepted allocation framework yet. But the raw material exists at the call level: Claude Code's OpenTelemetry export already emits [per-skill, per-plugin, per-subagent attribution on every call](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/monitoring-usage).

**The judgment: contain by capability with least-privilege boundaries, and commit a per-task cost schema at the call level *before* you need to enforce a budget.** Tier an agent's production authority by the blast radius of the task, not by trust in the model — a config change and a database migration should never carry the same token. Treat autonomy as an earned score you can revoke. Attribute cost per `(agent, task, user)` at emit time, because a dashboard tells you what you spent last month while a per-task ceiling can stop the next call. (Deep dives: [Tier Your AI Agent's Production Authority by Task Risk](/blog/agent-permission-tiering/) and [You Can't Cap What You Can't Attribute](/blog/per-task-cost-attribution/).)

## Pillar 5 — Team & Process

**The claim: reviewer-hours are the real ceiling on how many agents you can run, and the burden concentrates on the people you can least afford to lose.**

You didn't hit a correctness wall when you gave your team five agents — you hit a reviewer-throughput wall. Generation is largely solved; the wall is downstream. The number is already in your telemetry: across two years of data on 22,000 developers, [Faros AI found median time in review up **441.5%** and unreviewed merges up 31.3%](https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-acceleration-whiplash-takeaways) (vendor telemetry, correlational, lowest- versus highest-adoption periods). This is where our `/reports` caching PR actually lands — in a queue that has grown 441.5% slower, behind every other agent's diff, in front of a reviewer pool that didn't grow. Review time doesn't rise 441.5% because the code got worse; it rises because more of it arrives at a fixed denominator.

The load isn't uniform, which is what makes it dangerous. Agent PRs [split into a two-regime reality — "agents excel at narrow automation (28.3% of PRs merge instantly), but frequently fail at iterative refinement, leading to 'ghosting'"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.00753) ([Dao Sy Duy Minh et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.00753)) — creating what the authors call a hidden [*"attention tax" on maintainers*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.00753). The reflexive fix — a review agent — backfires when you measure it by comments instead of load removed: studied across thousands of real PRs, [CRA-only PRs merged at 45.20%, "23.17 percentage points lower than human-only PRs (68.37%)"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03196) ([Chowdhury et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03196)), because [60.2% of those PRs fell into the 0-30% signal range](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03196) — a [*"low signal-to-noise ratio"* when the agent is tuned to find everything](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.11078) ([Pereira et al., Nutanix, CR-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.11078)).

Meanwhile the real cost hides where dashboards don't look. Faros describes senior engineers becoming the verification layer, a burden that [*"does not get measured in PR throughput dashboards, which is precisely why the cost is invisible until it isn't"*](https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-code-quality-senior-engineer-review-burden), priced at [$150,000 to $300,000 per senior engineer who burns out and leaves](https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-code-quality-senior-engineer-review-burden). Much of that burden is [*"reconstructing intent from generated code, thin specs, incomplete Jira tickets, and edge cases nobody wrote down"*](https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-code-quality-senior-engineer-review-burden). The peer-reviewed corpus frames the same dynamic as externalization — one developer's gain becomes the team's uncounted cost: [*"The development time has been shortened but the team now needs to spend more time to review. Doesn't look like any benefit."*](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.27249v2) ([Baltes, Cheong, Treude](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.27249v2)).

**The judgment: budget review as a finite, protected resource, and split the delegable half from the half that can't move.** Do the reviewer-hours math *before* you approve agent number seven — past the crossover, [the review queue "becomes the binding constraint on their delivery pipeline"](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.13175v1) ([Monperrus](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.13175v1)), and each added agent buys queue depth, not throughput. Triage review depth by risk class, not author or diff size: a config change earns a linter and a glance; a payments path earns the full stack. Cap high-risk AI-diff load per reviewer and make it a line item in headcount planning. And split the role — line-by-line inspection is mechanical and delegable, but [*"the review, understood as judgment about whether the software is correct for its purpose, is relocating to where the agent cannot follow"*](https://blakecrosley.com/blog/agents-supersede-the-reviewer) ([Blake Crosley](https://blakecrosley.com/blog/agents-supersede-the-reviewer)) — to intent on the way in and accountability on the way out, because a model can't be paged for what it shipped. (Deep dive: [Review Capacity Is the Real Ceiling on Your Agents](/blog/review-capacity-agent-throughput/).)

## Pillar 6 — Architecture Decisions

**The claim: split to many agents for context isolation, not speed — and build-vs-buy is now a five-year ownership call.**

The multi-agent hype sells parallelism; the real justification is narrower. Anthropic's own multi-agent system earns its complexity because [*"Subagents facilitate compression by operating in parallel with their own context windows … before condensing the most important tokens for the lead research agent"*](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system) — separate windows, not raw speed, are the reason to split, and splitting is expensive: the same system [uses "about 15× more tokens than chats"](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/multi-agent-research-system). Cognition, reversing its own earlier position, lands on the same constraint: [multi-agent systems "work best today when writes stay single-threaded and the additional agents contribute intelligence rather than actions"](https://cognition.com/blog/multi-agents-working) ([Walden Yan, Cognition](https://cognition.com/blog/multi-agents-working)). Osmani's practical bound is [*"Three to five teammates is the sweet spot. Token costs scale linearly with team size."*](https://addyosmani.com/blog/code-agent-orchestra/) And the failure mode when you over-reach is organizational, not a model deficiency: a Berkeley taxonomy [*"identifies 14 unique modes, clustered into 3 categories: (i) system design issues, (ii) inter-agent misalignment, and (iii) task verification"*](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.13657) ([Cemri et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.13657)) — a readiness test you can fail with the best model on the market. (Deep dive: [When One Agent Stops Being Enough: The Isolation Gate](/blog/multi-agent-context-isolation/).)

The larger shift is about ownership, and it is where our caching PR ends its journey. Cheap generation shrank the cost of *writing* software, not the cost of *owning* it — [60% of a software system's lifecycle cost is maintenance, against a "measly 40%" for development](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/97-things-every/9780596805425/ch34.html) ([David Wood, O'Reilly](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/97-things-every/9780596805425/ch34.html), with the caveat that the share ranges 40–80% by system type). Cheap generation shrinks the 40% and can make the 60% worse: GitClear's analysis found [refactoring dropped "from 25% of changed lines in 2021, to less than 10% in 2024, while lines classified as 'copy/pasted' (cloned) rose from 8.3% to 12.3%"](https://www.gitclear.com/ai_assistant_code_quality_2025_research). Once the PR merges, the missing invalidation path is precisely the kind of AI-introduced issue that survives — in the wild, [22.7% of tracked AI-introduced issues "still survive at the latest version of the repository"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.28592) ([Liu et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.28592)) — and the duplicated cache logic is the refactoring that quietly stopped happening around it.

The deeper reason the tail is expensive is that it is a knowledge problem, not just a code problem. Goedecke, arguing from Naur, holds that [*"the core output of software engineers is not the program itself, but the theory of how the program works"*](https://www.seangoedecke.com/programming-with-ai-agents-as-theory-building/) — and that agents [*"can't retain theories of the codebase. They have to build their theory from scratch every time."*](https://www.seangoedecke.com/programming-with-ai-agents-as-theory-building/) The agent that wrote the cache holds no standing model of why it exists; you do, and that is what you are paying to keep.

**The judgment: gate multi-agent on context isolation, and price build-vs-buy on the five-year maintenance tail, gated on verification.** When you can verify and own cheaply, the calculus shifts toward build; when the ownership risk is existential, it stays buy — the pattern that holds up is to [retain "Buy as the primary option" for mission-critical systems of record, and make selectively at the periphery](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26482) ([David Klotz](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.26482)). Cheap generation made custom software cheaper to write. It did not make it cheaper to own — build-vs-buy stopped being a development-cost comparison and became an ownership-cost bet. (Deep dive: [Build vs. Buy Agentic AI: Ownership Is the New Decision](/blog/build-vs-buy-agentic-ai/).)

## What breaks next

The six pillars describe where the cost is today. Here is where I think the pressure moves over the next 12–18 months — the judgment layer that isn't in any single essay, offered as forecasts to argue with, not facts to cite. Each argues from named, sourced premises; the forecast is mine.

**Verification becomes a first-class engineering discipline with its own tooling and titles.** Right now verification is an unbudgeted tax paid by whoever clicks merge — DORA already [names it formally as a "verification tax"](https://dora.dev/ai/roi/report/), where time saved in generation is re-allocated to auditing. When the [most common agent-PR defect is a description that lies about the diff](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.04886), [benchmarks can be gamed to perfect scores](https://rdi.berkeley.edu/blog/trustworthy-benchmarks-cont), and [agent reviewers still catch only 15-31% of what humans flag](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.26130), the honest reading is Osmani's: even with a verifier sub-agent, [*"'done' is a claim and not a proof."*](https://addyo.substack.com/p/loop-engineering) I expect "prove this diff does what it says" to become a named function with owned tooling, the way SRE professionalized after ops stopped scaling by heroics.

**The review-capacity ceiling forces an org-chart change, not just a process change.** The [441.5% review-time explosion](https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-acceleration-whiplash-takeaways) and the [$150K–$300K senior-attrition cost it concentrates](https://www.faros.ai/blog/ai-code-quality-senior-engineer-review-burden) can't be absorbed by exhortation. Teams will either cap agent fleets to reviewer capacity explicitly, or lose the seniors who *are* the capacity — and that is a headcount-planning change, not a tooling one.

**Autonomy stretches faster than oversight, so containment beats monitoring.** Anthropic's own telemetry shows the gap widening by default: between October 2025 and January 2026 the [99.9th-percentile Claude Code turn duration "nearly doubled, from under 25 minutes to over 45 minutes"](https://www.anthropic.com/news/measuring-agent-autonomy), while [full auto-approve runs in ~20% of new users' sessions and "over 40% as users gain experience"](https://www.anthropic.com/news/measuring-agent-autonomy). Longer unattended turns and fewer approval gates mean the supervision surface shrinks as capability grows — so bounding what an agent *can* reach will beat trying to watch everything it does.

**Cost attribution becomes a governance requirement.** The raw signal already exists per call ([per-subagent OpenTelemetry attributes](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/monitoring-usage)); what's missing is [an accepted allocation framework for multi-agent output](https://www.finops.org/wg/finops-for-ai-overview/). Whoever standardizes `(agent, task, user)` attribution turns "we spent a lot on AI last quarter" into an enforceable per-task ceiling — and once regulated industries can't attribute agent spend, it stops being a FinOps nicety and becomes an audit line.

**Agent memory becomes the next constraint-mover.** Goedecke's diagnosis is that the missing capability is [*retaining* theories of the codebase](https://www.seangoedecke.com/programming-with-ai-agents-as-theory-building/), not building them in-session. Whoever ships durable agent memory moves the constraint again — and the teams that win the move are the ones who already treat the system around the model as the product, because that discipline is what survives when the model changes underneath it.

The meta-forecast under all five: attention stays fixated on the capability curve — the next model, the higher benchmark, the longer horizon — while the value accrues to the teams engineering the system around the agent. The 19% was never a model problem, and no model release fixes it.

## The one-line version

Stop tuning the agent; engineer the system. Here is the whole report on one screen — the `/reports` caching PR's full journey, one station per row.

| Pillar | Where the cost landed | The number that proves it | The move you make |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1 · Task Design** | Before the prompt — the choice to delegate | <10% agent success on tasks past ~4 hours of human work | Score verification cost and blast radius before you delegate |
| **2 · Agent Runtime** | In the window — what the agent reads to write the diff | 68% instruction compliance at 500 instructions | Budget context against a ~150–200 instruction ceiling |
| **3 · Evals & Verification** | In the gap between the diff and its description | 45.4% of flagged agent PRs describe work the code didn't do | Diff the description against the code; green build ≠ correct |
| **4 · Production Operations** | At the boundary — what the agent can reach and cost | 0.8% of actions irreversible; dashboards lag up to 30 days | Contain by capability; attribute cost per task before you cap it |
| **5 · Team & Process** | In the review queue — who absorbs the diff | +441.5% median time in review | Budget reviewer-hours; split delegable inspection from human judgment |
| **6 · Architecture** | After merge — what you own for five years | 22.7% of AI-introduced issues never get fixed | Split for context isolation; price the five-year ownership tail |

Generation got cheap; everything downstream didn't. The constraint moved off the model and onto the system around it — what you delegate, the context you budget, the verification you owe, the boundaries you enforce, the reviewer-hours you protect, and the ownership tail you carry. That is not a problem the next model release solves, because it was never a model problem. Stop tuning the agent. Engineer the system.
